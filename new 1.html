    This Picture History :

The history of pictures progressed from ancient optical concepts to modern digital imagery. The journey began with the camera obscura, a dark room with a pinhole that projected an inverted image, understood since antiquity. In the 18th century, scientists like Johann Heinrich Schulze discovered that silver salts darkened when exposed to light, but couldn't make the images permanent. The breakthrough came in the 19th century. Joseph Nicéphore Niépce created the first permanent photograph, View from the Window at Le Gras, around 1826. His partner, Louis Daguerre, later developed the daguerreotype, which produced sharper, though non-reproducible, images. In 1841, William Henry Fox Talbot patented the calotype, a negative-positive paper process that enabled multiple prints. Later innovations, such as Frederick Scott Archer's wet collodion process, further improved quality. In 1888, George Eastman's Kodak camera made photography accessible to amateurs with the slogan, "You press the button, we do the rest". Color photography followed, with the Lumière brothers' Autochrome plates released in 1907. The ultimate revolution, however, was digital. The first digital image was created in 1957, and the first digital camera prototype was built in 1975 by Kodak engineer Steven Sasson. By the 1990s, digital cameras became commercially available, eventually dominating the market and placing powerful cameras into virtually every modern smartphone.
